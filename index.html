San Francisco Crime Trends 2003–2026

Two decades of police data reveal clear patterns: most crime concentrates in a few categories, some neighborhoods improved dramatically while others stayed challenging, and the shape of crime changed in surprising ways after 2018. Here’s what the numbers really tell us.

How the Data Was Prepared

Figure 1. Frequency distribution of criminal incidents by category (2003–2025). Most incidents cluster in a small number of categories (tall peaks), while a long tail represents less frequent but often more serious offenses. We focused on the most relevant categories that drive the majority of public safety decisions.

The analysis combines two official SFPD sources: 2003–2018 and 2018–2025. To avoid duplicates, only the 2018 records from the second file were used. We selected the most impactful crime categories based on volume, severity, and policy relevance.

1. The Big Picture: Where Crime Clusters

Most incidents fall into a small number of categories (the tall peaks), but there is a long tail of less frequent yet serious crimes. This shape tells us that focusing resources on a few key categories can have outsized impact.

When we examine the distribution shape of all reported crimes, we see strong clustering — a classic pattern where a handful of categories dominate. This is the first insight for decision-makers: targeted action on these peaks produces the greatest results.

2. How Crime Changed Over Time

Annual incident counts for focus crimes (2003–2025). Notice the sharp drop around 2020 (COVID-19) and the partial rebound afterward. Property crime declined more significantly than violent crime in recent years.

The time-series shape shows clear trends: some categories declined steadily, others showed sudden drops or rebounds. These changes reflect shifts in policing, policy, and city life.

3. Where Crime Happens: The Geographic Story

Relative risk by San Francisco Police District. Darker areas indicate higher crime density. Some neighborhoods improved dramatically; others still need focused attention.

The geographic distribution is highly uneven. A few districts drive most of the volume, while others show very different shapes — from heavy tails of occasional serious incidents to steady low-level activity.
