The Pulse of the City: Mapping Two Decades of San Francisco Crime

Between 2003 and 2026, San Francisco experienced profound socioeconomic and urban transformation, evolving from an emerging global tech center into a city grappling with the multifaceted challenges of the post‑pandemic era. Yet beyond the headlines and public discourse, the most revealing account of this evolution is preserved in two decades of police incident records. Our longitudinal analysis demonstrates that criminal activity in the city is far from arbitrary; instead, it exhibits well‑defined temporal patterns and distinct geographic concentrations, underscoring the structured and systematic nature of crime across San Francisco.

Data Foundation & Quality Assurance<

To uphold the highest analytical standards, the engineering research team at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) employs a unified longitudinal dataset sourced from the SF Open Data Portal. Specifically, we integrate the “Police Department Incident Report: 2018 to Present.” dataset with the “Police Department Incident Reports Historical 2003 to May 2018.” dataset through a rigorous harmonization and standardization procedure. This process ensures a coherent and methodologically robust foundation for all subsequent analyses.

This report draws on publicly accessible data provided through the San Francisco Open Data Portal, specifically within its Public Safety section. As one of the city’s most valuable resources for residents, researchers, and policymakers, this platform offers comprehensive datasets that facilitate transparent analysis of long‑term crime trends.

The “Focus Crime” Filter: Given the extensive scope of the dataset—encompassing 2,900,115 recorded incidents across 52 distinct crime categories—the analyst team implemented a targeted filtering framework to identify the offenses with the greatest implications for public safety and social stability. The selection was guided by the following criteria:

  • Violence or threats to life
  • Severe property loss or economic impact
  • Indicators of organized or systematic criminal behavior
  • High‑severity disruptions to social order

The Anatomy of Urban Incidents

Our findings reveal a "tall peak" distribution. A staggering volume of activity is concentrated in Larceny/Theft (over 750,000 incidents since 2003), while more violent categories like Assault and Robbery maintain a consistent, albeit lower, frequency.

Figure 1: The distribution of crime categories shows that focusing resources on the "tallest peaks" can yield the greatest impact on city-wide safety.

Geographically, the story is one of district-level disparity. While districts like the Tenderloin and Southern drive the volume of property crimes, the "rhythms" of these crimes vary. Larceny often follows an economic clock (aligned with business hours), whereas assaults often follow a social-emotional clock (peaking during late-night hours).
