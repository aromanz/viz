The Pulse of the City: Mapping Two Decades of San Francisco Crime

Between 2003 and 2026, San Francisco transitioned from a burgeoning tech hub into a complex post-pandemic metropolis. Beyond the media narrative lies a structured reality preserved in millions of police records. Our analysis reveals that urban crime is not a sequence of random events, but a system of predictable temporal rhythms and spatial concentrations.

Data Foundation & Quality Assurance

To maintain the highest standards of analytical integrity, the research team at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has engineered a unified longitudinal dataset. This foundation was built by harmonizing two primary streams from the San Francisco Open Data Portal: the Historical Incident Archive (2003–2018) and the Contemporary Incident Reporting System (2018–Present).

The integration process utilized rigorous standardization protocols to bridge discrepancies in reporting taxonomies and software migrations. This ensured that the 2.9 million recorded incidents remain comparable across two decades, providing a methodologically sound basis for identifying strategic public safety trends.

The "Focus Crime" Strategic Framework

With 52 distinct crime categories in the raw data, effective governance requires prioritization. Our team implemented a targeted filtering framework to isolate 12 "Focus Crimes"—those with the highest correlation to economic stability and resident wellbeing. The selection criteria are defined as follows:

  • Human Capital Protection: Incidents involving direct violence or threats to life.
  • Economic Safeguards: Crimes resulting in severe property loss or significant fiscal impact on the private sector.
  • Structural Integrity: Indicators of organized, systematic, or non-random criminal behavior.
  • Urban Order: High-severity disruptions that threaten the social fabric of the city’s commercial and residential districts.

The Anatomy of Urban Incidents

When evaluating the univariate shape of San Francisco’s criminal landscape, our findings reveal a classic "Tall Peak" distribution. A disproportionate volume of total activity is concentrated within Larceny/Theft, which accounts for over 750,000 recorded incidents since 2003. This category acts as the primary statistical driver of the city’s overall crime profile.

In contrast, high-severity violent categories—such as Assault and Robbery—exhibit a "Stable Baseline" distribution. While their absolute frequency is lower than property crimes, their persistence across two decades suggests they are governed by deeply rooted social drivers rather than the transient economic fluctuations that influence theft.

Categorical Frequency Distribution. From a strategic governance perspective, this visualization demonstrates that resource allocation targeting the "tallest peaks" of the distribution—specifically property crime—offers the highest potential for reducing aggregate incident volumes.

Geographically, the story is one of district-level disparity. While districts like the Tenderloin and Southern drive the volume of property crimes, the "rhythms" of these crimes vary. Larceny often follows an economic clock (aligned with business hours), whereas assaults often follow a social-emotional clock (peaking during late-night hours).
