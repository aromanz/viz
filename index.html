San Francisco Crime Trends 2003–2026

Two decades of police data show clear patterns: most crime concentrates in just a few categories, some neighborhoods improved dramatically while others remained challenging, and the overall shape of crime changed in important ways after 2018. Here’s what the numbers really tell us.

Begin the Story →

How the Data Was Prepared

This report combines two official San Francisco Police Department sources: incident reports from 2003–2018 and 2018–2025. To avoid duplicates in 2018, we used only the records from the second file. We focused on the most relevant crime categories that represent the majority of incidents and public safety concerns.

The Big Picture: Where Crime Clusters

Figure 1. Distribution of criminal incidents by category (2003–2025). A small number of categories account for the vast majority of reports (tall peaks), while a long tail of less frequent but often more serious offenses remains. This shape guides where focused resources can have the greatest effect.

The distribution reveals a classic pattern: most incidents fall into a handful of categories, while many others occur far less often. Understanding this shape is the first step toward smarter, more effective public safety strategies.

How Crime Changed Over Time

Figure 2. Annual incident counts for focus crimes (2003–2025). Hover over lines to see exact values. Note the sharp drop during the COVID-19 period and the partial rebound afterward.

The time-series shape shows clear trends: some crimes declined steadily, others remained stubborn, and a few showed sudden changes around 2018–2020. These patterns reflect real shifts in the city.

Where Crime Happens: The Geographic Story

Figure 3. Relative risk by San Francisco Police District. Darker areas indicate higher crime density. Zoom in and click any district to explore which categories dominate there.

Crime is not evenly distributed. A few districts drive most of the volume, while others show very different shapes — from heavy tails of occasional serious events to steady low-level activity.
